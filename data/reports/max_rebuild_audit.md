## Verdict: what is trustworthy vs not

**Trustworthy now:**
- Saturation is controlled (contract_value/portability rarely hit 100; risk spread).
- Current Sixers are correctly held off the acquisition board.
- Stars / missing-contract players are correctly excluded from the realistic board.
- Every row carries explanation + provenance columns; Priority capped at 10.

**NOT yet trustworthy (drives the max rebuild):**
- **Contract semantics are vague**: a single `salary_millions` conflates cap hit, base salary, and AAV; free-agent status (UFA/RFA/option) is not modeled, so FA lanes are inferred only from `expiring` flags.
- **Acquisition feasibility is shallow**: a single 0-100 by type, with no feasibility tier or per-player reason that weighs role importance to the current team.
- **Expected roles are inflated**: too many players reach 'Star'/'Starter' minutes share off a minutes+quality blend rather than impact-gated role logic.
- **Role archetypes are coarse** (no point-of-attack defense, pull-up vs C&S split, OREB vs DREB split) and expected_role is not impact-gated.
- **No data contracts / schema validation** guarantee provenance columns exist.
- **No CSV export** with explanation columns yet.
- **Backtest is thin**: single outcome proxy, not split by board, limited baselines.

**Conclusion:** the board is internally consistent and non-saturated, but its *inputs* (contracts, feasibility, expected role) are not yet defensible enough for a real front office. The max rebuild must make contract semantics explicit, gate expected role by impact, deepen feasibility, and enforce schema/provenance contracts.
